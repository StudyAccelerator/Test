# Your personal Facebook plan: show up as a doctor, not a brand

**Date: 15 July 2026.** Built from your logged-in account (audited directly), a research pass on the mechanics of personal profiles vs Pages vs groups (Facebook Help plus sourced secondary reading), and a research pass on the individual educators who actually win on Facebook in the UK exam niche. This is the companion to the ranked groups list and the parent-conversion breakdown in this same folder. It replaces the "compare yourself to MyEdSpace" lens with the right one: real people with a face and a following.

The one-line answer: **stop operating as your Page. Operate as your personal profile with Professional Mode on, become the recognised helpful doctor inside the big parent groups, and open your own A-level parents group, which is a gap nobody has filled.**

---

## Part 1: Your two identities, and which to use

You have two things on Facebook and they are not the same kind of object.

- **"Dr Waleed Ahmad"** is a **Facebook Page** (I confirmed this from the inside: it has Monetisation, Ad Centre, Charity Manager and a Professional dashboard, which are Page-only tools). It has 0 followers and one post. Its bio is already good: "Helping A-Level students turn hard work into A*s | UK Foundation Doctor | 1,000+ students | The system schools don't teach | Founder of A-Level Accelerators."
- **"Waleed Ahmad"** is your **personal profile**. It is the identity that actually joined the medicine group and comments there as "Waleed".

**This is the source of the confusion you hit.** When a group said "you can't join as a Page", it was because you were browsing Facebook *as the Page*. I saw it live: on the UK Medicine Applicants group, with the Page active, Facebook showed "This group doesn't allow Pages to join. Switch to your main profile to join and interact", and the Join button was greyed out. Most UK parent groups block Pages on purpose, because Pages read as businesses and businesses are what they are drowning in.

So the rule is simple:

| Identity | What it is | Use it for | Do NOT use it for |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Waleed Ahmad** (personal profile) | A person | Everything in groups: joining, commenting, answering parents, and running your own group. Your public posts. | Nothing is off limits; this is your main vehicle. |
| **Dr Waleed Ahmad** (Page) | A business page | The searchable credential a parent finds when they Google you. The only thing that can run paid ads later. | Groups. It is blocked from most, and unwelcome in the rest. |

**Do not make a third "additional" profile.** Facebook lets you, but additional profiles cannot turn on Professional Mode (the exact feature you need), and any rule strike on one can take down the whole account. One personal profile, one Page. That is the correct setup.

**The switch you need to make today:** click your picture in the top right of Facebook, and if it is set to "Dr Waleed Ahmad", switch to "Waleed Ahmad". That is the profile you should be living in.

## Part 2: Turn your personal profile into a shop window

The mechanics research surfaced one feature that is close to purpose-built for what you are doing. **Professional Mode** on a personal profile adds a Follow button, and here is the part that matters: when you comment in a group, Facebook shows a "Follow" link next to your name to people who are not your friends. Every helpful answer you leave in a parent group becomes a follow opportunity, without anyone having to send a friend request. It also lifts the 5,000-friend cap (followers are unlimited) and gives you post insights. It does not restrict group membership and it does not retroactively expose your private posts (audience is still set post by post).

**Action: turn Professional Mode on** for the Waleed Ahmad profile (Settings, or the "Professional dashboard" prompt on your own profile).

Then treat the profile like a landing page, because that is what a parent sees the moment they click your name from a group comment. What a stranger can see:

- **Always public, no matter your settings:** your name, profile photo, cover photo, and the **Featured section**. Facebook forces the Featured section public and gives you no way to hide it. That makes it your single most valuable piece of profile property, because it sits right on the path from "helpful comment" to "who is this person".
- **Public only if you set each to public:** your Intro/bio line, your Work entry, your town, and any links.

Concrete changes, in priority order:

1. **Name field: keep it "Waleed Ahmad". Do not put "Dr" in it.** Facebook's name standards ban titles on a main profile, and a reported profile can be forced through ID verification that strips it. You lose nothing, because "Dr" belongs in the places below where it actually does work.
2. **Intro / bio line (about 130 characters):** who you help, plus the credential. Something like: "NHS doctor. I teach A-level students the revision system that turns hard work into A*s. 1,000+ students taught. Founder, A-Level Accelerators." The word "doctor" is the first thing a parent should read.
3. **Cover photo:** this is your hero banner. Put your face, "Dr Waleed Ahmad", "NHS doctor", and the promise on it. A parent decides in about two seconds whether you are worth reading.
4. **Featured section (the proof shelf, forced public):** three items. A testimonial or a photo of you teaching; a "Start here: free revision diagnostic" card; and one credibility item (the doctor-teacher story, or the 1,000+ students line). This is where "Dr" lives loudly.
5. **Work entry:** "Founder, A-Level Accelerators" linked to the Page. This is the one job the Page does well: it is the credential a curious parent clicks through to.
6. **Pinned post:** your founder story. The doctor who learned the system, sat the exams, and now teaches it. Written once, pinned, so every new profile visitor lands on it.

**The pattern across all of them:**

2. **The group is the asset, the Page is a noticeboard.** The big numbers are all group memberships, not page likes. Sabah Hadi's group is around 20.7K; her Page is 2,105 likes. Same person. The community is the funnel and the trust engine; the page just sits there.

Sabah Hadi: "a mum, writer and founder" who "guided her own child to Cambridge". 5. **Soft, multi-rail conversion.** Free group, then email list or WhatsApp, then a paid membership, course or app. Early-bird pricing, member discounts, and openly calling a promo an "unashamed plug". Nobody hard-sells inside their own community.

That is your unfair advantage, and it is wasted if you hide behind a faceless page.

## Part 4: What to post, and which group each post goes in

Two streams. Stream A is you showing up in other people's groups (the join list in the companion file). Stream B is your own posts on your own profile and, later, your own group.

**Stream A: in the big parent groups (from the ranked list).** You are not broadcasting here; you are being useful in public where the rules allow, and letting the Follow link and your profile do the rest.

- **The helpful answer.** When a parent posts a question (revision, stress, subject choices, "how do I help"), leave a genuinely useful two-paragraph reply as Dr Waleed. This is 80% of the work and it is what builds the Top Contributor badge (see Part 5). Best groups for this: GCSE resources UK, A-Level Tuition, the two home-ed support groups, Parents of Teenagers UK.
- **The tutor-request reply.** When a parent posts "looking for an A-level tutor", reply fast (see Part 6). Best groups: A-Level Tuition, Home Education Looking For Tutors UK, GCSE resources UK.
- **The occasional value post, only where allowed.** Where a group permits a genuinely useful post (11+ & GCSE & A Level Success UK invites "free sessions"; the home-ed groups allow one business day), post something the group cannot get elsewhere: a free workshop, or the diagnostic framed as a free tool. Never a link where links are banned; use the comment-to-DM trigger instead (Part 6).

**Stream B: on your own profile (and later your group).** These are the individual-winner formats, adapted to A-level and to you:

- **The exam-calendar utility post.** This is the format that travels, and A-level has a full calendar nobody is serving on Facebook: what results day actually involves (Thursday 13 August), how the appeals and remark process works, what Year 12 predicted grades mean and when they are set, UCAS deadlines, what to do the week before mocks. Olga Lynch built 30K followers on the transfer-test version of this. You can own the A-level version.
- **The founder story and method posts.** Diagnose-before-you-treat, the system schools do not teach, the honest "I sat these exams and learned the hard way". Your voice, your face.
- **The proof post.** A student result or reflection (within your no-grade-claims rule until August), a parent's words, a photo of you teaching.
- **The comment-to-DM value drop.** "Comment SYSTEM and I'll send your child's free revision diagnostic." Proven twice now in your own research: Sabah Hadi uses it, and the Jeehwan Ahn webinar post you flagged ran on exactly this mechanic.

## Part 5: The Top Contributor badge, and why it is worth chasing

Facebook awards a **Top Contributor** badge to the most active helpful members of a group, on a rolling roughly 28-day window. It shows next to your name on every post and comment you make in that group. It is not an algorithm boost; it is a trust signal, the closest thing a group has to a visible "this person is a known helper" label that a parent sees before they even click your profile.

You can typically hold it with **two or three genuinely helpful comments a week** in a single group. So do not spread thin. Pick two or three target groups from the list, show up in those consistently, and become a recognised name in them, rather than posting once in fifteen groups and being a stranger in all of them.

This is also the honest answer to "how long before this works". The tutor-business coaches who have done this at scale say two to three months of consistent presence before it reliably pulls clients. The badge is the mile-marker that tells you it is working.

## Part 6: How to handle a parent who comes asking (the moment that converts)

This is where the money is made, so handle it the same way every time.

1. **Reply fast.** The one hard number in the research: sales leads contacted within the hour are around seven times more likely to convert than those left longer. A parent's tutor-request post collects competing replies within hours. Turn on notifications for your two or three core groups so you catch these.
2. **Ask a question before you pitch.** Reply publicly with a clarifying question, not a sales blurb: "Happy to help. What's the main issue: is it that the marks don't match the hours, or a specific subject falling behind?" This does two things. It signals diagnosis over selling, which is your whole brand, and it starts a conversation that keeps the thread alive and near the top.
3. **Let the parent message you first. Do not cold-DM them.** This is a real mechanic, not politeness. A message from someone who is not your friend lands in the parent's hidden Message Requests folder with no notification, so a cold DM often is simply never seen. And most parent groups ban unsolicited DMs to members outright. So end your public reply with an invitation: "Feel free to message me and I'll talk you through it." When they message, the thread lands in your inbox normally.
4. **In the DM, diagnose, then offer the call.** Short exchange: what is going wrong, what they have tried, the child's year and subjects. Then offer the free 15-minute call (this is the human-conversation step every competitor uses and none can match with an actual doctor). No links in the first message; links are the fastest way to get a message auto-filtered.
5. **Never put a link in a first contact, and never paste the same message to ten people in a day.** Both trip Facebook's spam detection. Every message handwritten, one parent at a time.

## Part 7: The groups question, answered

You asked for a recommendation, not a menu. Here it is: **do both, in this order, and weight them differently.**

**Now (this is the priority): get into the big existing groups as your Professional-Mode personal profile and become a recognised helpful doctor in two or three of them.** This is fast, it is free, it needs no audience of your own, and it is where the parents already are. It is also how you learn their exact language and objections. Join the Tier 1 list, turn on notifications, answer questions, chase Top Contributor in your two or three chosen groups. Start today.

**Next (the bigger prize, slower): open your own A-level parents group.** The evidence is unambiguous that the group, not the page, is where the scale and the trust live, and that the A-level-parent group does not yet exist at scale. This is the Sabah Hadi model applied to your niche, with a credential she does not have. Practicalities from the research: name it for what a parent would search ("A-Level Parents UK: Results, Revision & University", not your business name), and seed the first members from assets you already own, which is your 444-person email list and the thank-you emails on the diagnostic and tracker. The engine that keeps a group alive is a **weekly live session** where you teach one useful thing; that is what every successful group-owner does and it is exactly what you, as a teacher, are built for.

**Do not chase being "endorsed" or "verified" by the big group owners.** It is not how any of the winners grew, the owners are competitors or protective gatekeepers, and it puts your growth in someone else's hands. Earn standing by being useful in their groups (which you are doing anyway in the "now" step), and if a relationship with an owner develops naturally, good, but do not build the plan on it. Build your own room.
