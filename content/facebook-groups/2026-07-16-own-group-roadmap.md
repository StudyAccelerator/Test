## Part 2: Posting versus replying (the honest weighting)

Do both. They do different jobs, and neither replaces the other.

- **Replying is the daily engine, and it is where the money is.** A parent who posts "looking for an A-level tutor" is a buyer standing in the street with their hand up. Twenty minutes a day across the request groups (the 69.9K group, A-Level Tuition, GCSE and A-Level UK tutoring, Home Education Looking For Tutors) is the highest-value time you can spend on Facebook. Your Lucy Albert reply is the template: name, credential, two diagnostic questions, invitation to message you. Keep doing exactly that.
- **Posting is the weekly compounding layer.** One post per group per week, maximum, and only in groups whose rules allow it. Posts make you a known face to thousands of lurkers; replies convert the handful actively asking. Your A-Level Tuition post and your Parents in UK post are both the right formats (a conversation question and a useful contrarian take); rotate those with the exam-calendar utility posts. One caution from the lead radar: your founder-story post in GCSE and A-Level UK tutoring is stuck in its pending-approval queue, and with one admin against 227 posts a day that queue is a black hole. Treat the firehose groups as reply territory, not posting territory, and never wait on a pending queue.
- **Never blast the same post to ten groups at once.** The composer lets you; the groups' rules differ, the audiences differ, and identical text across groups is a classic spam signal. Tailor the opening line per group and stagger across days.

## Part 3: Never missing a parent's request (the 20-comments problem)

Today's lead radar diagnosed this from inside your notification settings, and the diagnosis changes the fix. Your group notifications sit on "Highlights", which Facebook defines as friends' posts and suggested posts. No parent posting a tutor request is your friend, so Facebook was never going to tell you, and it never has. Worse: **push notifications cannot do "All posts" at all.** The push options in the dialog are Highlights or Off, full stop. There is no phone alert for every post in a group, so the fix was never a notifications setting. Four fixes, in order of impact.

**1. The lead radar: Facebook post search, swept twice a day.** Post search finds live requests in near real time, including in groups you are not a member of. The 16 July test caught a request 23 minutes after it was posted. Bookmark the sweep searches from the lead-radar file (`facebook.com/search/posts?q=looking%20for%20an%20A%20level%20tutor` and the maths and recommendation variants), run them with your morning coffee and once in the evening, ten minutes total. One habit to attach: parents cross-post the same request to several groups, so check the other copies before judging how crowded a thread looks.

**2. The four-group shortlist gets in-app "All posts", as the second net.** In-app notifications (unlike push) can do all posts, which feeds the bell counter whenever you open Facebook. Set it on exactly four request groups: I am looking for a tutor or I want to tutor in the UK, A-Level Tuition, GCSE and A-Level UK tutoring, Home Education Looking For Tutors (UK). You are in 81 groups, so anything beyond a shortlist buries the bell (three of your groups alone post 227, 307 and 146 times a day). You change the settings yourself: open the group, tap Joined, then Manage notifications. And on the iPhone, keep the pushes that do exist (replies and comments on your own threads) arriving on time: Settings, Notifications, Facebook: Immediate Delivery rather than Scheduled Summary, and Facebook allowed through whichever Focus mode runs during your day.

**3. Stop cold-DMing; make them message you.** If you have been sending the first message, this is probably the real reason "they don't even see it". A message from someone who is not a friend lands in the parent's hidden Message Requests folder with no notification at all. It is not buried under twenty rivals; it is invisible. Reply publicly in the thread, end with "feel free to message me and I'll talk you through it", and when they message you the conversation lands properly in both inboxes.

**4. Win on substance, not speed, and know the size of this prize.** You are a doctor on shifts; you will never reliably out-sprint the agencies that camp the firehose groups, where a request collects replies within about 15 minutes. You do not need to. Look at what those 20 to 30 fast replies actually are: interchangeable "DM'd you!" one-liners, some of them byte-identical brochures pasted across unrelated posts. Parents do not pick the first reply, they pick the credible one, and comments with likes and replies float upwards under Facebook's "most relevant" sorting anyway. A reply like your Lucy Albert comment posted at position 27 beats a "DM me hun" at position 2. And hold the honest measurement alongside it: most request volume is GCSE, which you do not sell, and A-level requests ran at roughly one an hour across the whole of UK Facebook on a mid-July day. This channel costs minutes, teaches you parent language, and occasionally lands a student. It is worth exactly that, and it is one more reason the owned group in Part 4 is the actual growth plan.

## Part 5: The group blueprint (ready to paste)

**Name: "A-Level Parents UK (Revision, Results Day & University Advice)".** Parents find groups by searching what they need, so the name is keywords, not brand. Today's search for "a level parents" returns nothing over 689 members; this name walks into that gap. Do not put "A-Level Accelerators" in the name.

**Privacy: Private, Visible.** Every big parent group on your list runs private (the 44.3K and 54.1K ones included) because parents write more honestly about their named children behind a join gate, and the entry questions only exist on private groups. Facebook lets a public group go private but not the reverse, so starting private keeps every option open.

**Entry questions (exactly three):**
1. What year is your child going into this September? (Year 12, Year 13, resits, other)
2. What's the biggest worry about their A-levels right now, in a sentence?
3. Optional: I write a short weekly email for A-level parents with the same advice I post here. If you'd like it, leave your email. Totally fine to leave blank.

Question 1 tells you what to write. Question 2 is a running archive of parents' exact language for every future post and ad. Question 3 is the email bridge; move opt-ins into MailerLite once a week by hand, tagged as coming from the group.

**Rules (five):**
1. This group is for parents and carers of A-level and GCSE students. Students: my free tools are at alevelaccelerators.com, and this room is for your parents.
2. No advertising. No tutors, tutoring companies, courses or services promoting themselves, in posts, comments or DMs to members. Instant removal, no warnings.
3. Never share another person's child's name, school or results. Your own child, share what you're comfortable with.
4. Be kind. Every parent here is doing their best with a stressed teenager.
5. I'm a doctor, but nothing here is medical advice. If you're worried about your child's mental health, their GP and school are the right doors, and I'll always say so.

**About text:**
"Run by Dr Waleed Ahmad: NHS doctor, former A-level student who learned to revise the hard way, and founder of A-Level Accelerators. This group is where I answer A-level parents' questions for free: revision that actually works, results day and appeals, UCAS and university choices, and what to do when the effort is there but the grades aren't. I run paid programmes and I'll occasionally mention them in clearly marked posts, never in comments and never by DM. Everything else is free, and the advice is the same either way."

That transparency line is load-bearing. Every other tutor in these groups pretends they are not selling; a doctor who says plainly "here is where I make my living, everything else is free" is instantly the most trustworthy person in the room, and it kills the "sneaky advertiser" report before it exists.

**Cover image:** built and in your Downloads folder (group-cover.png), brand purple with the group name and your credential. Source in content/graphics-src/fb-group/ if we rename the group.

**Pinned welcome post:** written to do three jobs: say who you are, point at the diagnostic as "start here", and ask the one question that starts conversations ("what year is your child in, and what's the current worry? I read everything"). Draft in the launch kit.

A year of trust plus results week is when an owned group prints: this is the week Sabah's and Olga's engines run hottest. Everything from Phase 0 exists to make this week enormous.

### The floor, the ceiling, and the kill criteria

- **Time floor:** 30 minutes a day (replies everywhere, one group post) plus one Sunday hour (queue the week from the pack, admin, the AMA replies). A brutal rota week survives on the queue alone; the pack means there is always two weeks of content loaded.
- **Never two silent weeks.** If life intervenes, say so in the group ("on nights this week, back Monday"). A doctor being honest about shifts reads as credibility, not neglect.
- **Kill criteria, reviewed mid-September:** if posts average under two comments and joins run under ten a month despite the reply engine working, we change the format (more AMA, more results-day utility, different questions) before adding effort. The group only fails if it bores parents; it cannot fail by being small.
