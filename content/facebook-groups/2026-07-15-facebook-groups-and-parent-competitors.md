# Facebook groups worth joining, and who is winning parents on Facebook

**Date researched: 15 July 2026.** Researched from inside Waleed's logged-in Facebook account (group About pages, live feeds, rules, member counts and activity stats read directly), plus the Meta Ad Library (GB, active ads) and a parallel fact-checked web research pass on the wider parent-conversion market. Every number in this document was read from the named source on the date shown. Nothing is estimated. Groups marked **partial** are private: their About page and rules were readable but their feed was not, so treat those entries as incomplete until joined.

No group was joined during this research. Join decisions, entry questions and rule acceptances are Waleed's.

## Part 1: The ranked group list

Ranking weighs three things against each other: whether the people in the group are his buyers (UK parents of GCSE and A-level students) or his users (the students), whether the group is actually alive (posts per month as shown on the group's own About page, not member count), and whether the rules let a tutor get anything out of it. A big dead group scores nothing. A strict no-promotion group can still score if the comment route is open and the audience is right.

**Season note:** mid-July is the post-exam lull and several groups are visibly quieter than their trend. Results day is Thursday 13 August and September is the re-engagement spike. Joining now means being an established member before both moments, which matters because several groups gate or delay new members' posts.

## Part 2: Who is winning parents on Facebook, and exactly how

The clearest finding from inside the platform: **nobody in this market wins parents with organic page posts.** MyEdSpace has 11K page followers and its latest post had 1 reaction when checked. Up Learn's Facebook has roughly 1.9K followers (12 July 2026, from the committed competitor research). Organic pages are shopfronts, not engines. The three models that actually work:

### Model 1: the paid machine (MyEdSpace, the one to beat)

MyEdSpace had **roughly 160 active ads in the GB Ad Library on 15 July 2026**. Read together they are a complete parent-conversion course:

- **Price anchor against private tutoring.** "From just £5 per hour" set against "£1,000s per term" for an equivalent private tutor. Every ad frames cost as the parent's objection and kills it first.
- **Named teachers with elite credentials as the product.** A flagship ad tells the story of one teacher (Louis Provis, Master's degrees from Oxford and Cambridge, 10 years teaching) and prices him at £5/hour. The same creatives run simultaneously from the brand page and from teacher pages ("Neil Does Maths with MyEdSpace", "Alex Does English with MyEdSpace"), because a person converts better than a logo.
- **Results statistics with a national comparator.** "60% of our students achieved GCSE grades 7 to 9, compared to only 27% nationally"; subject-level versions ("74% of Biology students achieved grade 7 or above, versus 44% nationally"). Every claim is paired with the national average so the parent does the maths. Caveat from the verification pass: their stats are mutually inconsistent across their own simultaneous ads (60% vs 27% in one, 56% vs 22% in another, 58% in a third), so the technique is proven but no single figure of theirs should be repeated as fact.
- **Stacked third-party proof.** "Trusted by 21,000 families", Trustpilot five-star counts that rise across creative generations (1,950+ in June ads, 2,150+ in July ads), "Best Online School 2025", "Homeschooling Product of the Year 2025/2026".
- **Risk reversal everywhere.** A 14-day no-quibbles money-back guarantee closes nearly every ad.
- **The CTA is a conversation, not a checkout.** "Book a no-obligation chat": they sell parents by phone, not by cart.
- **A cheap seasonal tripwire.** Summer School at £49 for every subject (£20 for one), "pay once, no subscription", launched in ads from 22 June. It converts hesitant parents into customers at pocket-money risk before the September upsell.
- **Parent-education webinars as lead magnet.** Ads launched 1 to 2 July 2026: "Calling all parents! Do you want to learn how to help your child in school?", a webinar hosted by co-founder Sean Hirons. They are teaching parents how to parent students, and collecting the leads.
- **Objection handling written for the parent, not the student.** "Your child asks questions in the chat, no camera needed", "monthly progress reports so you can watch their improvement".
- **Segmented funnels.** Separate landers and creatives for home educators (myedspace.com/home-educate), English, Maths, science.
- One new line worth noting: "We don't make promises. Our students get results", a direct positioning shot at guarantee-led rivals (Up Learn's "A*/A or your money back").

Their group play is instructive too: **"Neil Does Maths ~ MyEdSpace" is a private Facebook group with 19.1K members** built on the teacher's personal brand from February 2023, addressed "Interested Parents..." in its description. It currently has zero posts in the last month: they built the community, harvested it, and let it idle once paid acquisition took over. The mechanic (person-fronted group for parents) demonstrably reached 19K; the abandonment is their weakness, not proof the mechanic fails.

### Model 2: the free-trial retail machine (Explore Learning, MyTutor)

- **Explore Learning: roughly 85 active GB ads on 15 July 2026.** Hooks: "Award-winning tuition for ages 4-16", dynamic localisation (ads inject the local centre's city), deadline urgency ("save £75 until 18th July!"), a specific progress claim ("Just 2 hours a week... 7x more progress this summer"), and a single CTA: "Book a Free Trial Today". The free taster, a deadline and a local anchor is the whole machine.
- **MyTutor: roughly 21 active GB ads.** "Online tutoring... proven to boost grades in 6 lessons or more" (a time-boxed outcome claim), "100% of our tutors aced their GCSEs", "400,000+ five-star reviews", and notably a results-day creative already in rotation in July ("GCSE results are in! Looking to improve your grades?"). They pre-build the August moment.

### Model 3: the one-person value page (The Transfer Tutor App)

A Northern Ireland transfer-test prep app, one niche, one person's voice: **30K followers and "98% recommend (307 reviews)" on the page, checked 15 July 2026.** It grows by posting data-rich value graphics (three-year score analysis charts built from "thousands of parents who sent me their results") that Facebook's algorithm pushes to non-followers as suggested content, which is exactly how it appeared in Waleed's own feed unprompted. Monetisation is soft: a "20% Off Summer Special" post, links in bio. This is the model closest to what Waleed can execute this month with zero budget: niche authority graphics, parent-supplied data, review proof, seasonal offers.

### Who is NOT on Facebook (gaps worth knowing)

- **Medic Mind: zero active GB ads** (exact-phrase Ad Library search, 15 July 2026) and their own Facebook group dead since 2018. The medicine-applicant audience on Facebook (the 15.8K group above) is unfarmed by its most obvious commercial owner.
- **Atom Learning: no active ads found** via its page ID on 15 July 2026 (single check; 11+ ad spend is seasonal and September-peaked, so treat as a snapshot, not a verdict).
- **Up Learn, Save My Exams, Seneca, PMT:** per the committed competitor research (12 to 14 July 2026), all have Facebook presences between 2K and 31K followers and none treats Facebook as a primary channel. The A-level-specific parent space on Facebook has no dominant incumbent. The 11+ space, by contrast, has roughly 270 active ads from local operators fighting for parents, which shows what a mature parent-buying market on Facebook looks like: early-start urgency ("Is your child in Year 3, moving into Year 4? Now is the perfect time"), small-class scarcity, grammar-school aspiration.

### What sits behind MyEdSpace's ads (verified 15 July 2026)

The parallel research pass traced the funnel end to end and adversarially verified it. Three paths run from those 160 ads, all live on 15 July 2026:

- **Path A, the sales call.** "Book a no-obligation chat" lands on a booking page (checkout.myedspace.com) with a callback picker and a WhatsApp line, and booking hands over a free "UK Education System" PDF. The call is taken by a commissioned sales team: their own London job ad describes "calling warm leads (parents who have already engaged with our ads) and closing sales for educational courses priced between £80 and £1500", with six more sales roles open across South America (jobs.ashbyhq.com/myedspacecareers, seen 15 July 2026). The "no-obligation chat" is a phone sales floor.
- **Path B, the webinar.** The parent webinar ads route to a registration page fronted by co-founder Sean Hirons with three live sessions that week, a countdown timer, and an evergreen automated twin. Content: why the system fails families, what tutoring costs, then the pitch.
- **Path C, the low-ticket checkout.** Summer School (£20/£37/£49) and Exam Masterclasses (£59 to £149) sell directly, capturing email and phone for the backend.

Their published weaknesses are Waleed's openings: classes are uncapped ("hundreds of students together", cameras and mics off, chat only, per their own FAQ), their sibling discount contradicts itself between pages (10% on the FAQ, 20% on the shop), their results statistics contradict each other between ads, and their one-star Trustpilot reviews complain of poor parent communication even while every ad promises "monthly progress reports". A doctor-founder teaching small groups with real per-student attention is the structural opposite.

### Model 4: the group-to-customer machine (Think Academy, The 11 Plus Journey)

The parallel pass found the two strongest owned-group operators, both verified:

- **Think Academy UK** (TAL Education subsidiary; the parent company is NYSE-listed with FY2026 revenue of 3.01 billion dollars, confirmed via their investor relations). The most complete Facebook machine in the UK market: they run join-this-group ads continuously (since 26 March 2026), feed the free maths groups (about 19.3K members across them, single-source count), then convert through free dated assessment events, a free trial lesson, and a one-to-one feedback report delivered by a consultant with enrolment targets. Live seat counters ("3 Left", "Full") on their booking pages, a £30 booster camp as the low-ticket rung, and a Study Report to the parent after every class. Their grade claims failed verification (offer counts inconsistent between their own pages), but the machine itself is confirmed and plainly pays for itself or the join-group ads would have stopped.
- **The 11 Plus Journey (Sabah Hadi).** A one-parent business built entirely on a free Facebook group (about 20.7K members, single-source) monetised through a ladder from £5.99 products to a membership and bootcamps around £30 to £105. Her mechanics: every ad and post opens first person ("Hi, I'm Sabah, a parent, mentor and founder"), her own children are the proof, she sells to the parent as a peer rather than as a teacher, sometimes selling courses to the parent directly (a £55 course for parents), and she uses comment-to-DM plays ("Comment PUNCTUATION and I will send you the details") that had parents responding within the hour on the day checked. She is the proof that one person with no ad budget worth mentioning can own a five-figure parent community.
- Also confirmed: the largest single concentration of 11+ parents on Facebook, "Grammar School Entrance (11plus) info" (43.6K members, private, individual-run since 2017), operates as a rentable channel that tutoring businesses pay to promote inside. Groups themselves have become ad inventory in the mature parent markets.

**Nobody runs this model for A-level parents. The slot is empty.** Think Academy stops at GCSE maths, Sabah stops at 11+, MyEdSpace built a 19K parent group and abandoned it. An "A-level Parents UK" group owned by a doctor-founder has no incumbent to displace.

### The franchise psychology (Kumon, Kip McGrath)

The old-school centre networks convert parents with the same two moves, confirmed live in the Ad Library on 15 July 2026:

- **Kip McGrath's master line, confirmed verbatim across many ad variants: "If you feel your child could use some extra support, trust your instincts."** The whole pitch validates the parent's judgement so that booking the free assessment feels like good parenting rather than an admission of failure. Nobody in the A-level space talks to parents this way.
- **Kumon** (125.3K page followers, the biggest education page found) leads with struggle questions ("Is your child struggling in maths or English?"), sells confidence and habits rather than grades, anchors price per day ("from £2.45 per day"), runs dated free-registration windows ("until 31 July"), and converts through a free assessment meeting where the instructor presents a six-month study projection. Kumon-specific figures are single-pass research (their verification block was cut short), but the ad patterns were read live.

### Cross-cutting: what every winner does

Drawn from ten verified deep-dives (MyEdSpace, Explore Learning, Think Academy, The 11 Plus Journey, Kumon, Kip McGrath, Atom Learning, The Exam Coach, Up Learn, and the Grammar School Entrance group):

1. **The conversion event is always a human conversation**, and every free thing exists to cause it: MyEdSpace's chat, Kumon's assessment meeting, Think Academy's feedback report, Explore Learning's free trial (Direct Debit offered inside the session).
2. **The parent is addressed as the capable operator; the child is never blamed.** The system or the exam is the villain, the parent is the intervener.
3. **Confidence and relief are the emotional core; grade statistics are just the proof block.**
4. **Risk reversal is stated at every conversion point, every time** (14-day guarantee, cancel anytime, free assessment, no joining fee).
5. **Urgency is calendar-true and year-group-specific**, never generic: registration deadlines, mock windows, "sets are decided at the end of Year 9", dated offer expiries.
6. **A low-ticket rung (£20 to £59) bridges free to full price** (Summer School £49, £30 booster camp, The Exam Coach's £20 standalone workshops with "no camera or mic needed").
7. **The parent buys visibility**: progress reports, study reports after every class, projection meetings. The artefact of "you will see the improvement" is sold in the ads themselves.
8. **Where the business is small, the founder is the product** (Sabah); where it is big, named teachers are (MyEdSpace). The faceless corporates list it as their own weakness.

### Claims checked and killed (do not reuse)

The verification pass refuted or downgraded these, so this document deliberately avoids them: MyEdSpace's specific results percentages (internally inconsistent) and "21,000 families" (their own ads alternate between families and students); Think Academy's "1,045 top school offers" and "95% success rate" (unverifiable, inconsistent with their own older claims); Explore Learning's "21 months' progress in a year" and "7x more progress" (internal contradictions, no visible methodology). Kumon's fee figures are plausible but single-pass. Where a number above says "about" or "single-source", that is deliberate.

## Part 3: Three things they do to convert parents that Waleed does not, actionable this week

Waleed's Facebook baseline as of 12 July 2026: page essentially unstarted, 0 followers, one post. The standing channel work is Part 1 itself: join the Tier 1 groups, set notifications, and answer every relevant parent request post as Dr Waleed, because being in the room when a parent asks for help is the cheapest conversion mechanic on this list and the fastest way to learn parent language. Beyond that, three specific gaps, each startable this week without ad spend:

1. **Every winner converts through a free human conversation. His funnel stops at a PDF report.** MyEdSpace's "no-obligation chat" feeds a phone team closing £80 to £1,500 sales; Kumon converts in the free assessment meeting; Think Academy converts in the one-to-one feedback report call; Explore Learning converts inside the free trial session. The diagnostic currently emails a report and ends. This week: add "Book a free 15-minute results chat with Dr Waleed" to the diagnostic report screen and the first follow-up email, using the existing book-a-call link, written in the disarming register the winners use ("no obligation, we'll just look at what the results mean"). Pure copy change, and it is the one version of this play nobody can match: at every competitor the chat is with a salesperson; here it is with the doctor who teaches.

2. **The winners put a person's face and first-person story in front of parents, with a reply mechanic. His parent channel has neither.** Sabah Hadi built a business on "Hi, I'm Sabah, a parent, mentor and founder"; MyEdSpace fronts its co-founder and named teachers; Kip McGrath's whole pitch validates the parent's instinct ("If you feel your child could use some extra support, trust your instincts"). This week: publish a pinned founder-story post on the page ("Hi, I'm Waleed, an NHS doctor..."), plus two or three parent-language posts in the same instinct-validating register, each ending with a comment-to-DM mechanic ("Comment SYSTEM and I'll send your child's free revision diagnostic"). Sabah's version had parents replying within the hour; the mechanic works at zero followers because shares carry it.

3. **The winners state a risk reversal and dated, year-group-specific stakes at every conversion point. His pages state neither.** MyEdSpace never runs an ad without the 14-day money-back line; Explore Learning never shows a price without "cancel anytime, no joining fee"; urgency is always a real date (registration windows, mock results, "sets are decided at the end of Year 9"). This week: decide the guarantee or trial framing (one decision only Waleed can make), then publish it on /summer-accelerators and the diagnostic report page, alongside honest dated stakes that need no invention: results day is Thursday 13 August, Year 12 predicted grades are set in the autumn term, September timetables reward students who fixed their system in August.

Close behind, in order: **open his own "A-level Parents UK" Facebook group** (Think Academy and Sabah prove group-to-customer pays; every proven operator stops at GCSE or 11+; the A-level slot is empty and this research found no incumbent); a free parents' webinar once there is an audience to invite (MyEdSpace's co-founder webinar creative has run since July 2025 and was relaunched this month, so it demonstrably pays); per-hour price anchoring against the £40 to £100/hour private tutor benchmark; a public review asset (The Transfer Tutor App converts on "98% recommend (307 reviews)"); pre-built results-day creatives (MyTutor's are already live in July); and source-tagging every parent-facing link now so the first month of Facebook activity is measurable.
