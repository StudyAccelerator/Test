Session five: how to read a result. In medicine you're trained never to treat the number; you treat what's behind it. A result isn't a verdict. It's a data point, and data points get read before anyone acts on them.

The triage, for any result that stung (A-level, AS, or a mock you're still carrying):

1. **Split the disappointment from the data.** The disappointment is real. Have it, properly. But it can't read a mark scheme, so it doesn't get to make decisions.

2. **Get the breakdown.** The slip gives you grades; the marks per paper say far more. Your school's exams officer can see component marks, so ask which paper leaked. It's usually one, not all of them, and that changes everything about the fix.

3. **Classify before you fix.** For the leaking paper, four questions. Did I not know it? (Knowledge.) Know it but couldn't produce it from memory? (Recall.) Produced it but it didn't fit the question? (Application.) Ran out of time, misread, panicked? (Exam craft.) Four different diseases, four different treatments, and treating the wrong one is how students repeat a result.

4. **Then decide the big thing** (resit, insurance place, move on) with the classification in hand, not the feeling.

The resit question, honestly: a resit makes sense when the classification says fixable process (recall, application, craft, timing) and you have the year to train it. It makes much less sense when the honest answer is that you and the subject never got on and the route you want doesn't need it. Decide from the paper, not from August's pride.

**The clinic**

"I missed my firm by one grade and my insurance confirmed. Is a remark worth it?"

Ask a teacher this week whether your script was genuinely marginal; they can see how close the boundary was. A review of marking can move marks down as well as up, so it's for marginal scripts, not for hope. And ask yourself the harder question: if the review comes back unchanged in three weeks, will you wish you'd spent those weeks falling in love with the place that already said yes? Sometimes the strongest move is exactly that.
