Here's a sentence that would have changed my own Year 13 if someone had said it to me in August: predicted grades aren't predictions. They're conclusions. Teachers draw them from evidence, and the evidence window opens the day you walk back into school.

Session six: how predictions actually get set, and how to move yours.

Most schools settle UCAS predictions through late September and October, earlier if you're applying for medicine, dentistry, vet med or Oxbridge, because that deadline is 15 October. What goes into them: how you finished Year 12 (fixed, already banked), what your teachers see in the first weeks back (live), and the first proper tests of the autumn (live, and the heaviest lever for moving a prediction).

Understand the teacher's side and the whole game changes. A teacher who wants to predict you higher has to defend that number in a department meeting, with evidence. So your job isn't to persuade anyone. Your job is to hand them the evidence.

The evidence plan. One page, tonight, twenty minutes:

1. **Write down the prediction you need**, from the real entry requirements of the courses you want. Look them up. Not folklore, not what someone's cousin got in with.

2. **Book your Year 12 patch list into week one.** The three weakest topics per subject. If you've been running the audits from this summer's Sessions, you already have the list. If not: spec open, score every topic out of 5, anything at 3 or below is the list.

3. **Circle the first test date** for each subject the moment it's announced, and train for it like it's the real exam. Timed, book closed, marked meanly. For your prediction, it is the real exam.

4. **Two weeks in, take your best piece of marked work to each teacher** and ask one question: "What would my work need to look like for you to predict me a [grade]?" Write the answer down. Then do the answer.

That's the whole plan. The students who get the benefit of the doubt in October aren't the loudest ones. They're the ones whose first fortnight made the case in writing.

**The clinic**

"My teacher barely knows me. How do I get a fair prediction?"

Then the first month is your introduction, and you get to write it. Sit where you can be seen working. Ask one good question per lesson, the kind that shows you did the reading. Hand the first pieces of work in on time and properly finished. And bring them the marked-work question from move four above. A month of that turns "barely knows me" into "consistently impressive", and that's the phrase that moves predictions in meetings.

Reply with yours. One line is fine.

**High-yield / low-yield**

High-yield: treating the first test like the real exam. For your prediction, it is.

Low-yield: September optimism with no calendar behind it. Intentions don't get predicted. Evidence does.
