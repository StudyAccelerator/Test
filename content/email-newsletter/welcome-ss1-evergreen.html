Right. Session one. The map everything else hangs on.

When a student tells me "I'm doing everything and the grade won't move", I ask one question: which tier are you training? Almost nobody can answer, because nobody's ever shown them the tiers. Every mark you'll ever earn lives on one of four floors:

Tier 1: Knowledge. You understand the content when it's in front of you.

Tier 2: Recall. You can produce it from memory. Notes closed, nothing prompting you.

Tier 3: Application. You can use it on a question you've never seen before.

Tier 4: Exam mastery. You can do all of that on the day, to time, under pressure, in the language the mark scheme pays for.

School lives almost entirely on Tier 1. Lessons explain content. Homework checks content. And "revision", the way most students run it (re-reading, highlighting, making the notes lovely), is Tier 1 polishing Tier 1. Then the exam turns up and pays out almost entirely on Tiers 2, 3 and 4.

I fell straight into this myself. My A-level revision was re-reading my notes until they felt familiar, and familiar felt like knowing. It wasn't. The exam hall feeling of "I knew this yesterday, where's it gone" isn't a memory problem. It's a student who trained Tier 1 for two years and got tested on Tier 4.

Here's the useful part. The tiers stack, so your grade is set by the first weak tier, not the strongest one. Work below your weak tier feels comfortable and earns you almost nothing. Work at it feels awkward, and that's the tell. The discomfort is where the marks are made.

Tonight, run the ten minute audit:

1. Take one subject. Ask, honestly, tier by tier: could I explain this topic with the book open? Could I write it out with the book closed? Could I score on an unseen question about it? Could I do that in timed conditions?

2. Score each tier out of 5. The first tier that drops below 4 is your floor.

3. That floor is where your revision lives now. Not the floors beneath it. They're already yours.

Ten minutes per subject, and you'll know more about your own revision than most students find out in two years.

The clinic

A question I keep getting from students starting Year 13 in September: "Should I get ahead on next year's content over summer, or go back over Year 12?"

Audit first, then both, in that order. Year 13 content stands directly on Year 12, so a weak topic doesn't stay one weak topic: next year's material lands on top of it and the crack travels upwards. Fix your three weakest Year 12 topics per subject first, and fix them at Tier 2, not Tier 1: book closed, from memory. Then preview September's first topics. A student who does both walks in ahead twice.
