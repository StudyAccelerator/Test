Confession first. At 17 I owned four highlighters and I knew how to use them. My folders were beautiful. Colour-coded, underlined, margins ruled. I re-read those beautiful notes until every page felt like a friend, then sat my mocks and watched marks fall through my hands, and I couldn't understand it, because I'd "done the work".

Session three: what notes are actually for.

A revision note has exactly one job: to be the thing you rebuild from memory, not the thing you admire. The moment note-making becomes note-decorating, the effort quietly moves from remembering to formatting. Formatting pays zero marks.

Beautiful notes feel so good because they're visible progress. A finished page looks like learning in a way a messy recall attempt never does. That look is the trap. Copying and colouring are nearly effortless for your brain, and memory is built by effort. Easy in, easy out.

The fix costs one blank page tonight:

1. Take a topic you've "finished" recently. Book closed. Write everything you can remember: definitions, diagrams, equations, the lot. Ugly is correct. Speed over neatness, arrows over headings.

2. Open the book. Different colour pen. Add everything you missed.

3. Look at the page. The first colour is what you actually own. The second colour is your real to-do list, generated by your own memory, and it's worth more than any folder.

If you've been with me since the diagnostic, you'll recognise this as blurting. Good. Recognising it and doing it weekly are different things, and the version most students drift back to (notes first, admire, re-read) is the one that let me down in my own mocks.

And your existing beautiful notes? Don't bin them. Demote them. They're now the answer key you check the ugly page against. The checking is not the revision. The ugly page is the revision.

**The clinic**

"Do I need to finish my notes before I start past paper questions?"

No, and the waiting is costing you. Notes and questions aren't stages, they're a loop: attempt, find the gap, patch it, attempt again. A student six topics deep in ugly recall pages beats a student with one immaculate folder, every single time. If it helps, steal last week's move and let the questions come first.
