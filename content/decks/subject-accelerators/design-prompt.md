# Subject Accelerators · deck brief

I need a slide deck designed. Here is the job, every fact it has to carry, and the brand it lives in. The design itself is yours.

I run A-Level Accelerators, a doctor-led A-level education business in the UK. This deck sells the Subject Accelerators, our live 12-week exam programmes in Biology, Chemistry and Maths, on a call with a parent (usually with their teenager sitting next to them). I present this live, sharing my screen, and I do not have long. The deck has to get the key points across fast and look genuinely premium: the kind of deck that makes us look like a serious operation. It is a sales tool, not a lesson. Nothing on it should teach, pad or explain at length.

**Deliverable:** 9 slides, 16:9.

## The audience

Parents of a Year 12 or Year 13 student who is stuck in one or more subjects, typically 40 to 55. The child works hard and the grades do not move, which is the frustrating kind. Most of these parents have already tried a tutor: it felt productive, they liked them, nothing changed. So the central objection this deck has to beat is "how is this different from the tutor we already paid for?"

The answer, which the deck must make obvious: a tutor re-explains content the student has already been taught. We train the thing that is actually losing the marks, which is applying knowledge under exam conditions and answering the way the mark scheme rewards.

## The brand

A-Level Accelerators is a doctor-led A-level education business. The look is calm, precise and expensive: closer to a private clinic or a good university press than to a tutoring ad. It should feel engineered rather than decorated, and it should never look like a school worksheet or a startup pitch template.

The live site is the reference and it is public: **alevelaccelerators.com**. Look at it. Match the family, do not clone the pages.

**Palette.** These are the whole palette. No other colours.

- Deep purple `#2E2557` (the anchor: dark backgrounds, headings)
- Purple light `#3d3370` (depth on dark only)
- Cream `#FBF8F3` (the light background, and text on purple)
- Cream dark `#F3EBD8` (quiet fills)
- Gold `#C9A96E` (the single accent, used sparingly and with intent)
- Gold light `#e0b577` (gold on dark, where plain gold goes dim)
- Ink `#1a1535` (body text on light)

Gold is an accent, never a body-text colour and never a background wash. If a slide has gold in three places, it has gold in two places too many.

**Type.** Headings and numerals in a serif (the site uses Georgia; a better serif of the same character is welcome). Body in a clean sans. Small labels in a mono face, uppercase and letterspaced: that mono label is the brand's "engineered" signal and is worth keeping.

**The signature move.** A single phrase inside a headline set in italic serif and gold, while the rest of the headline stays purple or cream. It is on the homepage ("Top grades are a *system*, not a talent."). One per slide at most. It is the one flourish the brand has; everything else earns its place by being clear.

**Texture.** Flat brand shapes, generous whitespace, soft layered shadows rather than hard borders. Real photography only where photography is used, never stock or clipart. No emoji anywhere. No gradients beyond a subtle wash.

**Tone of the design.** Every slide should land in about three seconds: the point arrives before the viewer reads the body text. This is shown on a shared screen during a live call, often on a laptop, sometimes over Zoom compression, so small type and low-contrast greys fail.

## The slide plan

One idea per slide. Each line is the job that slide has to do, not a layout: how you get there is your call.

1. Name the programme, land the promise (twelve weeks to turn a subject around), and put the cohort date in front of them.
2. The whole offer in six facts, price included, so that if the call ends here the pitch has still landed.
3. Beat the tutor objection: hard work is not the problem, and this is not more tutoring. Contrast what we train against what typical tutoring does.
4. Who built this: a doctor who learned the hard way, plus the tutors' credentials. This slide is where trust is won.
5. The method in three steps: diagnose, rebuild, coach.
6. What a week actually looks like: the session, the worksheet, the Q&A, the recordings, and the weekend timetable that never clashes.
7. Proof: the confidence numbers with their honest captions, and the two student quotes.
8. Pricing: all three options, the two-subject one flagged as best value for most, the tutoring comparison and the risk-free guarantee.
9. The decision: what happens next, the cohort date, how to enrol, and the guarantee once more.

## Copy rules

The words on the slides are in my voice, so:

- British English. Direct, short, emphatic. Contractions are fine. Fragments are fine; filler is not.
- **Zero em dashes or en dashes anywhere.** Write "4 to 6 hours", never "4-6" with a dash character. Use commas, full stops or colons instead.
- No AI-tell vocabulary (delve, crucial, robust, leverage, seamless, comprehensive, unlock, elevate, moreover, furthermore, landscape). No "not just X, but Y". No rule-of-three padding.
- No invented statistics, and no claims about the grades our students achieved: we have no results data yet, so any outcome claim is off the table. The proof list above is all we have and it is enough.
- Never describe us as matching students with tutors. I built the method and my team teaches it.
- Testimonial quotes stay verbatim.
- Slides carry fragments and statements, not paragraphs. If a fact will not fit, leave it out: I will say it.
