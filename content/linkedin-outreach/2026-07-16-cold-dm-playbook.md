# LinkedIn cold outreach playbook: diagnostic-first

Written 16 July 2026. The channel doc for Waleed's 1:1 LinkedIn outreach to A-level students. Replaces the old approach (cold offer of a 30 minute call), which converted near exam season and died in summer: a half hour call with a stranger costs a teenager social energy, scheduling and the risk of being sold to. The diagnostic costs 4 minutes, feels like a personality quiz with substance, and ends with something personal about THEM. That is a cold-offer shaped object. The call moves DOWN the funnel: it gets offered after the diagnostic, to people who now know the report and trust the voice.

Standing rule, same as Facebook and Reddit: **Waleed sends every message himself.** Sessions draft and refine; they never connect, message or reply as him.

## The funnel

1. Connection request (short note, or blank)
2. M1, the warm opener: one genuine question, zero links, zero offer
3. Their reply decides the bridge: reflect it, give one useful reframe, then the diagnostic with the profile-curiosity close ("tell me which one you get")
4. If they take it: talk about their profile like a doctor discussing results. THEN the call exists as a natural next step, free, no pressure
5. If no reply: exactly one gentle follow-up 3 to 4 days later, then leave them alone

The link appears once, at step 3, never earlier. In DMs use the tracking link so GA4 shows what the channel produces:
`https://alevelaccelerators.com/revision-diagnostic/?utm_source=linkedin&utm_medium=dm`

## Who to message

Year 12s heading into Year 13 (the Summer Accelerator window), and ambitious Year 11s starting A-levels in September. A 17 year old with a LinkedIn profile is self-selected ambitious: usually medicine, dentistry, law, Oxbridge, computer science. Thirty second profile read before any message: headline (aspiring what?), school year, subjects if listed, last post if any. That read decides which opener variant and gives the personalisation word. Skip anyone who looks under 16 or clearly finished with A-levels.

## The messages

Every message gets rephrased slightly per person. Never send an identical sentence to ten people in a day: it reads fake to the receiver and looks like spam to the platform. These are skeletons, not scripts.

### Connection notes (pick one, or send blank)

**V1, aspiring medic or dentist:**
> Hey [name], saw you're aiming for medicine. I'm a foundation year doctor who sat A-levels not that long ago, and I teach students the revision side now. Thought I'd connect.

**V2, general ambitious Year 12:**
> Hey [name], good to see a Year 12 actually using LinkedIn. I'm an NHS doctor and I teach A-level students how to revise properly. Happy to connect.

Blank requests are fine too: the profile does the talking, and accept rates for students are high when the headline is credible.

### M1, the opener (after they accept)

Goal: any reply at all. One question, easy to answer, calibrated to their profile. No links. No mention of the diagnostic.

**V1, general Year 12:**
> Thanks for connecting, [name]. Honest question, I ask every Year 12 I meet: now the dust has settled, how did Year 12 actually go? You're allowed to say "could've gone better". That's the most common honest answer.

**V2, aspiring medic:**
> Thanks for accepting, [name]. Always good to meet a future medic. Where are you up to with it all: UCAT booked, personal statement started, or still recovering from Year 12 like a normal person?

**V3, they post content:**
> [name], thanks for connecting. Saw your post about [topic], [one genuine specific sentence about it]. Out of interest, what's the plan for the summer before Year 13?

### The bridge (their reply decides which)

Pattern: reflect what they said, give ONE reframe that is useful on its own, then the diagnostic, closed with profile curiosity. The close matters: "tell me which one you get" keeps the conversation alive after the link.

**They say "fine, but [subject] was rough":**
> That's the most common answer I get, and it usually means the effort is there but some of it isn't scoring. I built a free tool for exactly this: 20 questions, about 4 minutes, and it works out where your marks are leaking and which of 7 revision profiles you are. Genuinely curious which one you'd get: [link]

**They say it went badly or they're worried:**
> Rate the honesty, most people pretend. Here's the thing though: a rough Year 12 is nearly always a method problem, not an ability problem, and method problems are fixable by September. Quickest way to see what's actually broken is my free diagnostic. 4 minutes, tells you which of 7 revision profiles you are and the one thing to fix first: [link]. Tell me what it gives you.

**They say it went well:**
> Love that. Then the real question is whether your method survives Year 13, because the content roughly doubles and "went well in Year 12" is exactly where strong students get ambushed. I built a free 4 minute diagnostic that scores how you actually revise across 5 systems. Even top students usually find one leak. What profile do you reckon you'd get? [link]

**Aspiring medic talking UCAT and personal statement:**
> All of that matters, but predicted grades are the quiet one: they get set this autumn from your first Year 13 tests, and your offer sits on them. Before September's the time to find out where your revision leaks marks. My free diagnostic does it in 4 minutes, and it tells you which of 7 revision profiles you are: [link]. The profile is usually the interesting bit.

### No reply? One follow-up, 3 to 4 days later, then stop

> No stress if you're switched off for the summer, [name], that's allowed. One thing before I leave you to it: I made a free 4 minute diagnostic that tells you which of 7 revision profiles you are and where your marks leak. Worth 4 minutes of a summer day: [link]. Either way, good luck with Year 13.

### After they take it

> Go on then, which profile did it give you?

Then talk about their result like a doctor discussing findings, two or three messages of genuine back and forth. Only then:

> If you ever want to go through the report properly, I do a few free half hour calls a week, and I'll tell you straight what I'd do in your position. No pressure though, the 7 day plan in your report is the main thing. Run that first.

### If they say no thanks, at any point

> All good, [name]. Good luck with Year 13, and if revision ever stops cooperating you know where I am.

Then stop. No exceptions.

## Mechanics

- 10 to 15 new conversations a day, maximum. Every one handwritten from the skeletons, no two identical.
- One thread. Never stack a second message before a reply, except the single scheduled follow-up.
- Teen reply windows: evenings and weekend afternoons. Send M1s in the evening, bridges whenever they reply.
- The link appears once per conversation. If they lose it and ask again, that's a buying signal, send it again happily.
- Under 16 or clearly GCSE: don't pitch anything, be kind, move on.
- Tone check before every send: would an NHS doctor be comfortable with a parent reading this over their child's shoulder? It's the older-brother-who-became-a-doctor voice, never a salesman.

## The profile is the landing page

Most students will open Waleed's profile before replying. Two upgrades that make every message warmer:

1. **Headline** should name the free thing: e.g. "NHS doctor · I teach A-level students where their marks leak · free 4 minute diagnostic below".
2. **Featured section** should pin the diagnostic (link plus the share-card image). A student who checks the profile can now find the tool without being sent it, which converts the silent lurkers the DMs never hear from.

## Measurement (weekly tally, no guesses)

Track: requests sent, accepts, M1 replies, links sent, completions (GA4 sessions and generate_lead conversions on utm_source=linkedin, plus MailerLite joins that day), profile-reply conversations, calls booked. If accepts are high but M1 replies are low, the opener is the problem. If links are sent but completions are low, the bridge oversold or the person was the wrong fit. If completions are high and calls are zero, that is fine: the email sequence is doing the rest of the job.
