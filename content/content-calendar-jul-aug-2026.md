### Day 2 | Wed 9 July
**LinkedIn** (authority/technique)
The most dangerous revision advice that goes round every exam season:

"Just do as many past papers as you can."

It's not wrong. It's incomplete, and done the usual way it's nearly useless.

Most students do a paper, check the mark scheme, see the score, and move on. That tests them. It doesn't teach them anything.

The mark scheme isn't an answer key. It's a map of exactly how an examiner thinks.

The version that actually moves grades: do the paper, close the mark scheme, write your answers. Then for every lost mark, ask three things.

1. What exact phrasing did the examiner want?
2. What structure did the model answer follow?
3. What would I need to know to produce that from nothing, under time?

That analysis is slow and uncomfortable. That discomfort is where the marks are made.

### Day 3 | Thu 10 July
**LinkedIn** (authority/reframe)
"Knowing something" and "being able to perform with it in an exam" are two completely different skills.

Your child can read a chapter, nod, and genuinely understand it. Then sit a paper on the same topic and freeze.

Nothing went wrong with their memory. They trained recognition (seeing it and going "yes, I know this") when the exam tests retrieval (producing it from nothing, under pressure, against a clock).

Recognition feels like learning. It's the comfortable option. It's also why so many hard-working students plateau.

The fix isn't more hours. It's swapping re-reading for testing themselves from memory. Same time, completely different result.

### Day 4 | Fri 11 July
**LinkedIn** (authority/framework)
Most students think there's one thing standing between them and an A. There are actually four, and they stack.

Tier 1, Knowledge. Do you understand the content? (This is all school and most tutors cover.)
Tier 2, Recall. Can you produce it from memory, no notes?
Tier 3, Application. Can you use it on an unfamiliar question?
Tier 4, Exam mastery. Can you perform on the day, to time, under stress?

Here's the problem. Most students spend ~95% of their effort on Tier 1, the one they've usually already got. That caps them at a B.

The A and the A* live in Tiers 2 to 4, which almost nobody is deliberately training.

Find the tier your child is stuck in and you've found the grade.

### Day 7 | Mon 14 July
**LinkedIn** (authority/technique, introduce blurting)
The single highest-return revision technique I know, and it needs nothing but a pen and paper.

It's called blurting.

You study a topic for a few minutes, close everything, and write down every single thing you can remember on a blank page. Then you open your notes and, in a different colour, add everything you missed.

Those gaps in colour are the entire point. That's your revision to-do list, generated by your own brain, showing you exactly what you can't yet recall.

Most students revise by re-reading, which hides the gaps. Blurting exposes them. It's uncomfortable, it's fast, and it's how medical students get through the largest content loads in education. I used it myself.

### Day 12 | Sat 19 July
**LinkedIn** (technique, spaced repetition)
Why your child forgets a topic two weeks after revising it, and the fix.

Memory fades on a curve. Revise something once and a big chunk of it is gone within a fortnight unless it comes back. This isn't a flaw in your child. It's how every human brain works.

The fix has a name: spaced repetition. Instead of revising a topic once and moving on forever, you revisit it at growing intervals. Learn it, test it the next day, again a few days later, then a week on.

Each visit takes less time and the memory lasts longer. It's the difference between a revision plan that leaks and one that holds.

A good weekly timetable builds this in automatically. Most homemade ones march through the syllabus once and never look back, which is a plan for forgetting things in a tidy order.

### Day 14 | Mon 21 July
**LinkedIn** (technique / interleaving)
A small change to how your child revises that feels harder and works better: mixing subjects instead of blocking them.

Most students revise in blocks. All of Monday on biology. All of Tuesday on chemistry. It feels organised and it's the weaker way to do it.

Mixing topics and subjects across a session (called interleaving) forces the brain to keep choosing the right method for the right problem. Which is exactly what an exam paper does to you. It never tells you which chapter a question is from.

Blocked revision feels smooth and confident. Mixed revision feels messier and harder, and produces better recall. In learning, a bit of difficulty is usually the sign it's working.
*Hashtags: #ALevels #StudyTips*
[VISUAL: "blocked vs interleaved" comparison card.]

**Facebook** (parents)
Counterintuitive revision tip: mixing subjects in one session beats doing one subject all day.

It's called interleaving. Jumping between topics forces the brain to pick the right method each time, which is exactly what an exam makes you do. It feels harder than nice tidy blocks, and that difficulty is the point. It's what makes it stick.

If your child revises in big single-subject blocks, suggest mixing it up. Feels worse, works better.

### Day 17 | Thu 24 July
**LinkedIn** (technique / how many hours)
"How many hours a day should my child revise?" is the question I get most. It's also slightly the wrong one.

The honest answer: in the holidays, around two focused hours a day is plenty. In exam season, four to six. Almost never more, because past roughly four hours of genuinely hard mental work, quality falls off a cliff, even for elite performers.

The 10-hour revision day a stressed teenager announces is usually four hours of real work stretched across ten hours of guilt. Plus exhaustion, minus the sleep that actually locks memory in.

What matters far more than the hour count is what fills the hours. Two hours of testing themselves beats six hours of re-reading. Every time.

So the better question isn't "how long", it's "doing what".

Exam confidence isn't a personality trait your child either has or doesn't. It's built, and it's built from evidence.

A calm student in the exam hall usually isn't calmer by nature. They've sat that exam before, in mock form, several times, and they know exactly what they'll do if their mind blanks.

### Day 26 | Sat 2 Aug
**LinkedIn** (technique, shareable, keeps value flowing)
A revision rule that sounds too simple to matter: revise the topics you're avoiding.

Every student has topics they quietly skip. The ones that feel hard, or boring, or just never quite get picked. And because we all drift toward what feels good, they revise their strong topics again and again, and the weak ones stay weak.

But your child already has the marks in their strong topics. The grade they're missing is sitting almost entirely in the ones they avoid.

So the highest-return revision instruction is almost annoyingly basic: rate every topic honestly, then spend most of your time where it's uncomfortable. The avoided topics are the grade.

Ten days to results day. Let's demystify the word that scares families most: clearing.

Clearing is not a failure system. It's how tens of thousands of students find a university place every single year, including students who did brilliantly and simply changed their minds.

If your child misses their firm and insurance offers, clearing is the route to a place, and it moves fast on results morning. The families who handle it well have done three things in advance: made a shortlist of realistic backup courses, found the clearing hotline numbers, and agreed that your child (not you) makes the calls, because universities want to speak to the applicant.

Knowing how clearing works removes most of the fear from results day. It turns "what if it goes wrong" into "here's exactly what we'd do".

### Day 30 | Wed 6 Aug
**LinkedIn** (technique, keeps authority flowing pre-results)
The night before an exam (or a results day), the most useful thing your child can do is often nothing.

There's a strong pull to cram the night before. It almost always backfires. New information doesn't lodge usefully at that point, and the cost is the sleep that consolidates everything they already learned.

The evening before is for light review at most, then a proper night's sleep. On results morning it's the same principle: the outcome is already set, so the job that day is staying steady and thinking clearly, both of which need rest, not panic.

Preparation is what you do over weeks. The 24 hours before is for protecting the preparation you've already done, not cramming in more.
