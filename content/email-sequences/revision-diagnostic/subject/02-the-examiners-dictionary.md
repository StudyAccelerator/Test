Did the drill from my last email? If so, you've now seen your letters: K, R, W, T. And if your log looks like most students with your diagnostic, the W pile, knew it, wrote it, worded it wrong, is the tall one.

So today, the treatment for W. It starts with a reframe that changed how I revised for every exam I've sat since:

A mark scheme is not an answer sheet. It's a foreign language dictionary, and the exam is a translation test. You think in your own words. The examiner can only award marks for theirs. Every W in your log is a sentence you translated badly, and the fix is the same as any language: vocabulary, deliberately learned.

Here's how you build the dictionary in {$diag_worry_subject|default('your subject')}.

**One: collect the phrases**

Open three or four mark schemes for papers you've done or topics you know well. Go through with one question: what exact wording keeps being rewarded? Every subject has these. In biology, answers live and die on words like "complementary", "specific", "gradient". Chemistry schemes reward precise mechanism language and die on vague arrows. In maths, "hence" in the question is an instruction with marks attached, and method marks have their own grammar entirely.

Write the recurring phrases into the back pages of your error log, sorted by topic. Ten minutes per scheme. Within a fortnight you'll have a personal dictionary of what your board actually pays for, in their exact dialect.

**Two: learn it actively, like vocab**

Reading the dictionary does nothing. This is language learning, so treat it that way: cover the phrase, produce it from memory, say it out loud. Out loud matters more than you'd think. Phrases you've spoken come back under pressure in a way phrases you've silently recognised never do. Two minutes at the start of each session in that subject. That's the whole habit.

**Three: decode the command words**

Separate from content vocabulary, there's the grammar of the question itself, and most students have never once looked it up, even though every board publishes it.

"State" wants a fact, no reasoning, stop writing. "Explain" wants cause and effect chained with becauses, and the marks live in the links. "Compare" wants both things in every sentence, and answers that describe them in turn score half. "Evaluate" wants a judgement at the end, and a surprising number of evaluate answers lose the final mark by never actually judging.

Before you write a single word of any answer, name the command word and say what it's asking you to produce. Five seconds. It's the difference between answering the question and answering the topic, and examiners only pay for the first one.

**Four: close the loop**

From now on, every W in your error log gets two lines instead of one: your wording, and theirs, side by side. Reading your own sentence next to the paying version is uncomfortable in a very productive way. The gap between the two lines is usually tiny, a missing "because", a vague verb, and that's precisely the point. You were one phrase from the mark. Repeatedly. That's not a knowledge problem, and now you can see it isn't.

I'll be honest about the ceiling of doing this alone, because your report deserves honesty. Self-marking has a blind spot: you, marking you, will sometimes give yourself the benefit of the doubt, and the benefit of the doubt is exactly the thing the real examiner doesn't give. There's a version of this training where a specialist reads your actual answers, in real time, and tells you precisely which phrase would have earned the mark, week after week until it's automatic. That's what our live subject programmes are for, and I'll lay that out properly, including the honest costs and who shouldn't bother, in a couple of days.
