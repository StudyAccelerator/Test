{$name},

Your diagnostic said something specific about you, and I want to make sure you actually heard it, because it's unusual and it changes the prescription completely.

Your revision engine is broadly working. The leak it found is {$diag_bottleneck|default('Exam Craft')}: the conversion step between what you know and what the examiner reads. In {$diag_worry_subject|default('your weakest subject')}, you're currently working at {$diag_current_grade|default('your current grade')} and aiming for {$diag_target_grade|default('higher')}, and my honest read of profiles like yours, and I've seen a lot of them across 1,000+ students, is that a decent chunk of that gap is already in your head. It's just not landing on the page.

Frustrating? Yes. But hear the good news in it: content takes months to build. Conversion is a skill, and skills train fast. You're the fixable case.

Here's the training. It's one drill, done properly, once or twice a week.

**The examiner drill (about 80 minutes)**

Part one, 30 to 40 minutes: sit one section of a past paper in {$diag_worry_subject|default('your subject')} under real conditions. Real timing, scaled to the marks (about a minute per mark, check your board's ratio). No notes. No pausing to check one thing. No music. The conditions are the point: an exam tests retrieval under pressure, so practising without pressure is practising a different sport.

Part two, 10 minutes: before touching the mark scheme, go back through your answers and mark, in the margin, every question where you felt that little wobble of "not sure I said that right". Trust the wobble. Calibrating your own uncertainty is itself an exam skill, and it's trainable.

Part three, 30 minutes, the part that separates grades: mark it with the official scheme, and mark it meanly. You're not your friend here, you're the examiner, and the examiner gives nothing for "they clearly sort of knew it". The scheme says specific things earn the mark. If your answer gestured at the idea but missed the wording, no mark. Count it properly.

Then, for every dropped mark, write one line in your error log with a letter next to it:

K: I genuinely didn't know it. (A content gap. These go on your revision list.)
R: I knew it but couldn't produce it in the moment. (A retrieval gap. Blurt that topic this week.)
W: I knew it and wrote it, but not in the scheme's language. (A wording gap.)
T: I ran out of time or misread. (A conditions gap.)

Now look at the distribution of letters. Students with your diagnostic result almost always discover the pile is mostly W and T, with far less K than the panic suggested. That distribution is the most useful thing you'll learn about yourself this month, because it tells you where the next hour of revision should go, and it's almost never "make more notes".

Part four, non-negotiable: rewrite every W answer, in full, in the exact language the scheme rewards. By hand. Then say it out loud. This feels ridiculous. It's also how the phrasing becomes yours instead of the scheme's, and it's where the drill actually converts into marks.

One student's version of this stuck with me. Maahil, one of our chemistry students, described the approach as "we go straight into exam practice instead of spending ages on content, and it works". That's the entire philosophy in one sentence: at your stage, with your profile, the paper is the textbook.

Do the drill once this week. One section, one honest marking, one letter-coded log. Next email I'll show you the layer under it: the examiner's dictionary, and how to learn mark scheme language the way you'd learn actual vocabulary, because that's what it is.

Keep going!

Waleed
