Every September, the same thing happens. Thousands of students walk into Year 13 believing Year 12 went fine, because the mock happened months ago and the painful bits have gone quiet.

Then week three arrives. The new content lands on top of the old content, teachers say "you covered this last year", and the students who "did fine" discover that fine was doing a lot of heavy lifting.

Here's the uncomfortable truth about A-levels: Year 13 doesn't test Year 13. The final exams test everything, and the questions that separate grades love reaching back into the material you learned when you were still finding your feet. A gap you ignore this summer doesn't stay the same size. It compounds, because half of next year's topics stand on top of it.

Your diagnostic flagged {$diag_bottleneck|default('your revision method')} as the system leaking most, and when I see that pattern in a Year 12 student in July, the prescription is always the same: before you fix how you study, find out precisely what needs studying. Diagnose before you treat. So this email is the autopsy. It takes about 40 minutes and it's the single highest-value thing you can do with this week.

**The Year 12 autopsy, step by step**

Step one. Get the specification for {$diag_worry_subject|default('your weakest subject')}. Not your notes, not a revision guide: the actual spec from the exam board website. It's the only complete list of what they're allowed to ask you.

Step two. Go through every Year 12 topic on it and give each one a score out of 5, with the book closed. Not "have I seen this" but "could I answer exam questions on it tomorrow morning". A 5 means yes, comfortably. A 3 means I'd survive the easy marks. A 1 means I'd be praying it doesn't come up. You already know most of these scores in your gut. The audit just makes you say them out loud.

Step three. Be suspicious of your 4s. This matters. When you read a topic name and think "yeah, I know that", your brain is often recognising the topic, not recalling the content. Recognition feels identical to knowledge right up until an exam asks you to produce something. For any topic you rated 4, try this: close everything and write six lines about it from memory. If the pen slows down by line two, it's a 2, and it goes on the list.

Step four. Everything rated 3 or below goes on one page, ranked worst first. That page is your summer. Not "revise chemistry", which is a mood, but "fix electrode potentials, then rates, then buffers", which is a plan.

Step five. Count the topics, then count your remaining summer weeks. Divide. That number, topics per week, is the honest workload. Most students who do this get a small shock, and July is exactly the right month to get it. The same shock in October costs a grade.

When I did my own version of this at your age, I found eleven topics I'd been quietly avoiding for months. I remember the specific feeling of writing "electric fields" on the list and realising I'd been turning the page past it since February. Writing it down was uncomfortable for about four minutes. Carrying it silently had been uncomfortable for four months.

Do the audit tonight or tomorrow. It's 40 minutes, it costs nothing, and every email I send you after this one works better if the list exists.

One honest note before I go. The audit tells you what's broken. It doesn't rebuild anything by itself, and a list of 14 weak topics with no structure behind it is how a summer quietly disappears. Structure is fixable, and it's exactly what I'll show you in the next email: the daily shape that gets a topic properly rebuilt in 90 minutes without eating your holiday.

Keep going!
