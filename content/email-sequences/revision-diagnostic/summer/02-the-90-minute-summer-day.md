Now you've got the avoid-list from the autopsy, the next question is obvious: what do you actually do with it, in July, when nobody's setting homework and the days have no shape?

Here's what most students do. Nothing for eleven days, then one guilt-fuelled five hour Sunday of re-reading, then nothing again. I know because that was me. The five hour day feels heroic, and it's almost worthless, because reading old notes for five hours builds recognition, and your exams don't test recognition. They test whether you can produce it, from memory, under time.

You told the diagnostic you're studying {$diag_hours_per_week|default('a fair few')} hours a week. I'm not going to ask you for more hours. I'm going to ask you for 90 minutes, most days, pointed at the right work. In a six week summer that's more than fifty focused hours, which is more real work than most of Year 12 contained. And it leaves your afternoons completely, guiltlessly free, which matters, because a student who burns their summer arrives in September worse off than one who rested properly.

**The 90 minute day**

Minutes 0 to 10: the blurt. Take today's topic from your avoid-list, worst topics first while your energy is fresh. Blank page, book closed, write everything you know about it. Diagrams, definitions, half-remembered fragments, all of it. It will feel bad. The page will look emptier than your ego expected. That page is the most useful document you'll produce all day, because the white space on it is a map of exactly what you don't know.

Minutes 10 to 55: rebuild. Now open the notes or the textbook, and fill the gaps in a different colour. You're no longer reading generally, you're hunting for the specific things your blurt page couldn't produce. Reading with a target is a different activity from reading to feel busy. Where the topic has calculations, do worked examples by hand. Where it's essay-based, build the skeleton argument.

Minutes 55 to 60: break. Leave the room. Phone stays where it was, which, since your diagnostic answers went in, we both know should be another room entirely.

Minutes 60 to 85: prove it. Exam questions on the topic, mark scheme afterwards, and every dropped mark written into an error log with the corrected answer next to it. Wrong answers you've corrected by hand are the highest-yield revision material you own. They're personalised by definition.

Minutes 85 to 90: book the returns. This is the step everyone skips and it's the one that makes the rest permanent. Memory fades on a curve, so a topic touched once resets to nearly zero within days. Write in your plan: re-blurt this topic tomorrow (ten minutes), then again in four days (ten minutes). Twenty extra minutes total, and the topic stops leaking. Skip them and you'll be re-learning it in October, paying full price twice for the same content.

That's the whole system. One topic properly rebuilt per day, two tiny returns riding behind it, phone in exile, afternoon free.

I'll be straight with you about the hard part, because your report was honest with you and I should be too. The method above is not complicated. Nobody fails it because they couldn't understand it. Students fail it because on day four it's raining, motivation is a liar, and there's no lesson, no teacher and no other students expecting you to show up. Self-taught summers die of loneliness, not difficulty.

That's the actual reason I built the Summer Accelerator the way I did: two live sessions a week per subject, where the deep work and the exam questions happen with a specialist steering and other Year 12s in the room. The structure arrives from outside, so your discipline is only responsible for the days in between. I'll tell you properly what it is, and just as importantly who shouldn't join it, later this week. Nothing you need to do today.

Today: one topic, 90 minutes, off the worst end of your list. Then close the books and go enjoy the sun. That's the system working, not you slacking.

Keep going!
