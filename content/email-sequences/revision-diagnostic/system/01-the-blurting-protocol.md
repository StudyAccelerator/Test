{$name},

Your report flagged {$diag_bottleneck|default('the way you revise')} as the system leaking most, and I want to start the treatment with the single method that attacks that leak hardest. It's called blurting. You may have heard the name on TikTok. Almost nobody does it properly, and the difference between doing it properly and doing it for the camera is the whole result.

First, the reason it works, because you'll stick with a method you understand and abandon one you're taking on faith.

When you re-read your notes, the content becomes familiar. Familiarity feels exactly like knowledge from the inside. Same warm feeling of "yes, I know this". But the exam never asks whether you recognise the cardiac cycle when you see it. It hands you a blank answer box and asks you to produce the cardiac cycle from nothing. Recognition and retrieval are different skills, stored differently, trained differently, and school only ever trained the first one. That gap is where "I revised loads and it went badly" comes from. It's a retrieval failure, not a knowledge failure, and honestly, not a you failure either. Nobody taught you.

Retrieval only gets stronger one way: by retrieving. Every time you force something out of your memory with the book shut, the pathway to it thickens. Blurting is retrieval, industrialised.

**The protocol**

One: pick a single, specific topic. "Biology" is not a topic. "The cardiac cycle" is. Smaller than feels natural is right.

Two: blank page, book closed, phone in another room (your diagnostic answers and I both know where it usually is). Write everything you know about the topic. Definitions, diagrams, mechanisms, equations, fragments you're not sure about. Ugly is correct. Speed matters more than neatness. Five to ten minutes.

Three: stop, open the notes, and mark your own page like an examiner. Everything you got right, tick. Everything missing or wrong goes back on the page in a different colour. That second colour is the entire point of the exercise. It's a personalised map of your ignorance on this topic, on this date, and no revision guide on earth can sell you one.

Four: the part everyone skips. Close everything again and re-blurt just the second-colour material, from memory, right now. Not tomorrow. The gap between "I saw the correct version" and "I produced the correct version" closes in that second pass or it doesn't close.

Five: keep the page. Date it. You'll re-blurt this topic tomorrow for ten minutes, and again in about four days. I'll show you how to place those returns without willpower in the next email, because there's a trick to it: your calendar does the remembering, not your discipline.

**The two rules that keep it honest**

Every session ends with the book closed. Whatever you studied, the last ten minutes are you reproducing the core of it from memory. If you can't produce it now, three metres from your notes, you could not produce it in an exam hall in June, and it's better to find out tonight.

And every wrong answer earns a line in an error log. One page per subject: the question, your wrong answer, the corrected one next to it. Retest the log on Sundays. Wrong answers you've corrected are the highest-yield material you own, because each one is a mark you've already paid for once.

That's it. No highlighters, no four-hour aesthetic note sessions, no productivity apps. A pen, an ugly page, and the discomfort of finding out what's actually in your head. That discomfort is where the marks are made.

Tonight: one topic, one blurt, both colours, the second pass. Twenty-five minutes, all in. Then reply to this email with one word describing your first blurt page. Mine, age 17, would have been "humbling".

Keep going!
