### Mon 20 | three questions that work better

If asking your teenager about revision starts an argument, try changing the question.

"How's revision going?" gets a "fine" and a closed door. It asks them to defend themselves.

These three get you real information instead:

1. "Which topic are you avoiding at the moment?" Every student has one, and it's usually where the grade is hiding.

2. "When you revise something, how do you know it's actually gone in?" If the answer is "I read it and it makes sense", that's the gap. Reading feels like learning. Exams test remembering.

3. "If the exam were tomorrow, which paper would worry you most?" You'll learn more from this than from any school report.

None of them mention hours. Effort is almost never the problem.

Confession from a doctor who did fine in his A-levels: my Year 12 mocks were genuinely bad.

What saved me wasn't working harder. It was one question my mum asked, mostly out of frustration: "show me HOW you revise, then."

Nobody had ever asked me how. Teachers checked homework. Reports tracked marks. But the actual method, me alone at a desk re-reading notes until they looked familiar, nobody had ever watched it happen. Ten minutes of watching told her everything.

Question for the parents here: have you ever actually watched how your child revises? Not how long. How. And if you tried it tonight, what do you think you'd see?

**Draft D · A-Level Tuition · Thu 23**

Unpopular opinion from someone who marks the aftermath every summer: six hours a day of the wrong revision is worth less than ninety minutes of the right kind.

I'm an NHS doctor and an A-level tutor, and the hardest conversations I have are with families where the hours were all there. The desk time happened. The method quietly wasted it: re-reading, highlighting, producing beautiful notes. It looks like work. Exams don't pay for it.

If you're enforcing "hours at the desk" this summer, you're probably, kindly, measuring the wrong thing. Measure output instead: pages produced from memory with the book shut, questions attempted, marks recovered from last week's mistakes.

Push back if you disagree, I'm genuinely interested in how parents see it. And if anyone wants the ninety-minute version spelled out, ask below and I'll write it out properly.
