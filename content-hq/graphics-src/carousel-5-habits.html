A-LEVEL ACCELERATORS
1

Re-reading your notes.

It feels like learning. It isn't. The page looks familiar, so your brain says "I know this."

That's recognition. The exam tests recall. They are different skills, and only one of them earns marks.

THE FIX

Close the book. Blank page. Write what you actually remember. That gap you just found is your real revision list.

A-LEVEL ACCELERATORS
2

Highlighting everything.

A fully yellow page is not a revised page. Highlighting marks where the information lives. It does nothing to put it in your head.

I had textbooks that looked like sunflowers. My recall didn't improve one mark.

THE FIX

One question in the margin per paragraph instead. Tomorrow, answer them from memory before you reopen the book.

A-LEVEL ACCELERATORS
3

Building the perfect timetable.

I spent whole evenings colour-coding mine. Such a waste of time. A beautiful plan is the most respectable way to avoid actual revision.

The timetable isn't the work. The work is the work.

THE FIX

Plan tomorrow only. Three blocks, weakest topic first. Ten minutes of planning, maximum, then start.

A-LEVEL ACCELERATORS
4

Rewriting notes so they look nice.

Copying is not encoding. You can transcribe a whole chapter with your brain switched off, and it will feel wonderfully productive.

Neat notes are a nice souvenir. They are not marks.

THE FIX

Turn every heading into a question. Answer the questions from memory. Ugly and effective beats beautiful and useless.

A-LEVEL ACCELERATORS
5

"All of biology in 16 minutes" at 1am.

The night-before speedrun video is comfort, not revision. You're watching someone else recall the content. Their marks will be excellent.

Watching football has never made anyone fit.

THE FIX

If it's the night before, do past paper questions on your weakest topics and sleep. Recall beats replay, every time.
