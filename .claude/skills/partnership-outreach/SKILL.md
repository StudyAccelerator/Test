# Partnership research and outreach | A-Level Accelerators

Waleed grows through partnerships as well as paid acquisition. The play: established education companies with big audiences, offered something they do not currently have. This skill turns a company name into four things he can act on the same day:

1. A deep, honest picture of the target's business.
2. One specific opportunity worth putting in front of them, pressure-tested rather than assumed.
3. A named decision maker with a route to reach them that Waleed can verify.
4. A draft email a busy founder would open, read to the end, and reply to.

Read `references/assets-and-trades.md` before designing any offer. It holds the approved credibility record, everything Waleed can put on the table, what he wants back, and the red lines.

## Non-negotiables

1. **Nothing is ever sent.** No emails, no contact forms, no DMs, no comments. Every output is a draft for Waleed to approve, edit and send himself.
2. **No invented numbers.** Every figure about the target carries its source. Estimates are labelled as estimates, with their basis and the date retrieved. If something cannot be known from outside (list size, revenue split), write that instead of guessing.
3. **Research reaches Waleed before the email is final.** He often knows things about a target that research cannot see. In an interactive session: present the dossier, ask what he can add, then finish the email. In an autonomous session: deliver the dossier plus an email clearly marked DRAFT, with the short list of questions only he can answer.
4. **The contact is a named decision maker**: founder, managing director, or head of partnerships or marketing. Someone who could personally say yes. Generic inboxes and contact forms are fallback routes, never the plan. Never fabricate an email address: it is published, verified, or a labelled inference with the evidence for the pattern.
5. **The email exists to get a reply and a call.** It never explains the whole business.
6. **No grade-outcome claims.** None exist until August 2026 results. Do not paper over that gap or hint around it. The delivery record in the reference file is the credibility, and it is enough.
7. All content rules apply: British English, zero em or en dashes anywhere (write "15 to 20 minutes"), straight quotes, no AI-tell vocabulary, no rule-of-three padding. The email must be in Waleed's spoken voice: read `.claude/skills/content-studio/references/brand-voice.md` before drafting it. Run the dash, curly-quote and banned-word scan on every output file before committing.

## The process

### 1. Frame the target

Check `content/partnerships/README.md` for earlier work on this company; never restart from zero. Create `content/partnerships/<company-slug>/` for the outputs: `research.md` (the dossier) and `outreach-email.md` (the draft plus send plan).

### 2. Research the business (the dossier)

Every claim gets a source next to it. Establish:

- **What they sell and what is free.** Products, prices, formats, dates. How the free layer feeds the paid layer.
- **Scale, honestly sourced.** Check their advertising or media page first: a company that sells ads publishes its real traffic, and that is the best number you will get. Companies House for UK companies: directors, incorporation date, company type (limited by guarantee means a genuine non-profit, which changes what motivates them), latest accounts. Third-party traffic estimates (Similarweb, Ahrefs when the session's plan allows) labelled as estimates with a retrieval date. Social followings counted on the platforms where possible.
- **Audience.** Who uses them, who pays, which country, students or parents or teachers.
- **Strategy signals.** Blog categories and cadence, newsletter existence and cadence, hiring pages, awards, recent launches, and whether outside brands already appear on their pages (precedent that they say yes to partners).
- **The gap.** What their audience needs next that they do not provide. Pressure-test it before claiming it: search their site and blog for it first. If they half-do it with a few articles, say exactly that, and build the offer on the difference between covering a topic and teaching it. A gap only survives if their audience plausibly wants it, they do not do it well, Waleed can genuinely deliver it, and it connects to something the target visibly wants.
- **Conflict check.** Map where their revenue overlaps Waleed's. Any offer that reads as "promote my competing product to your audience" dies on arrival. Design around the overlap and name it honestly in the dossier: the target will spot it even if the email does not.

### 3. Design the trade

Full creative freedom on the shape. The default is never "let me run my programme for you": that is one shape and often not the best one. Start from what they want and what they are short of, then find the trade where Waleed's assets fill it. Sketch at least three shapes before choosing a lead offer and a fallback. The currencies on both sides are listed in `references/assets-and-trades.md`.

Pressure-test the lead offer in writing:

- Why would they say no? Answer the objection in the dossier before Waleed hears it on a call.
- What does it cost them in effort, risk and brand? The best offers cost the target almost nothing beyond promotion.
- Why Waleed and not someone bigger?
- Why now? Peg the offer to the UK academic calendar (results day, the September habits window, mocks, Easter) so the email has a deadline built in.
- Can one practising doctor with a hospital rota deliver it? Never promise scale he cannot staff.

### 4. Name the decision maker

Find the person who can say yes to this specific trade. Founder-led company: the founder. Larger organisation: the head of partnerships, marketing or courses. Evidence trail: Companies House officers, the target's about and team pages, LinkedIn, press bylines, event listings. Deliver name, role, evidence of the role, and routes ranked by likelihood of being read, each with a confidence label:

- A published or verified direct email is gold.
- A pattern-inferred email (a contact database lists one, or published staff addresses reveal the pattern) is usable if labelled unverified with its basis, and Waleed verifies it before sending.
- A LinkedIn DM or connection note works when the person is visibly active there; include a note version under 300 characters.
- Contact forms and generic inboxes are follow-up routes after silence, never the opener.

### 5. Draft the email

The email gets a call, nothing more. Its structure is the give-first framework in `references/email-framework.md`, distilled from Daniel Priestley, Alex Hormozi, Mark Tilbury and Steven Bartlett. Read that file before drafting; it is the default for all outreach. The short version:

- **Do the work first.** The offered thing exists before the email does (a one-page outline, a plan with dates), and it goes in the output file as a ready-to-send appendix.
- **The five moves, in order: Gift, Receipts, Trade, Window, Ask.** Open with their win and the thing you are giving them. Never open with their problem or gap: that analysis stays in the dossier and surfaces on the call.
- Receipts are one or two lines of names and numbers from the approved record. Never the CV, never a grades claim.
- The trade carries the risk removal (they approve everything, they keep the assets, no selling) and kills the obvious objection in one clause.
- One small ask (15 minutes, he fits around their diary) followed by the easy no ("one line back and I'll leave it there").
- Under 160 words, Waleed's voice throughout (read `.claude/skills/content-studio/references/brand-voice.md` first), two or three subject options of three to six plain words.
- Deliver alongside the email: the made thing as an appendix, a LinkedIn note version under 300 characters, the day 4 and day 10 follow-ups per the framework, and a pre-send checklist for Waleed (verify the address, send morning midweek from Waleed@alevelaccelerators.com, one link maximum, log the send in the README index).

### 6. Deliver

Write both files, add or update the target's row in `content/partnerships/README.md`, and end with a summary that leads with: the opportunity in two sentences, the named person and best route, the questions only Waleed can answer, and exactly what he does next. Where a load-bearing number is an estimate, say so again in the summary.

## Done when

- Waleed could act on it the same day.
- The opportunity is one he could genuinely deliver and one the target would recognise as worth their time.
- The contact is a named decision maker with a route Waleed can verify himself.
- The email is one he would be happy to have his name on: a busy founder would read it to the end and reply.
- Nothing was sent, posted or submitted anywhere.
