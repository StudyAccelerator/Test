# Blueprints

Three skeletons for the narrative you hand to Claude Design. Each lists the facts to collect before you write a word, and the job of each slide.

Slide jobs are written as outcomes ("land the whole offer in six facts"), never as layouts ("six white tiles in a 3x2 grid"). Keep them that way when they go into the prompt. Following a blueprint does not make a prompt right: the facts still have to be collected fresh every time, because a deck built on last month's price is worse than no deck.

## 1. Programme sales deck (9 to 10 slides)

A live call selling a paid programme to a parent, usually with the student there. The parent is the buyer, so credibility and safety carry as much weight as the offer.

**Collect first:** start date, duration, session cadence and length, subjects, exam boards, who teaches, recordings policy, clash policy, every price and every saving, the guarantee wording, what happens after the programme, the current approved proof points, two testimonial quotes that match the subject.

**Slide jobs:**

1. Name the programme, the promise and the start date.
2. The whole offer in six facts, price included. If the call dies here, the pitch has still landed.
3. Why this exists: the cost to the student of doing nothing.
4. Who built it: Dr Waleed MBBS, 1,000+ students, and the tutors' credentials.
5. The method: diagnose, rebuild, coach.
6. What is actually inside it: the shape of a session or a week, and what is included.
7. Proof: approved numbers with honest captions, plus verbatim quotes.
8. Pricing: every option, the one most people take, the value anchor, the risk-free line.
9. The decision: what happens next, the date, how to enrol.

## 2. Workshop deck (7 to 9 slides)

Pitching or opening a one-off workshop (school, parent group, online event).

**Collect first:** date, venue or platform, length, capacity, price or free, the audience, and the promised takeaway. If the workshop does not exist yet, all of that comes from Waleed and none of it may be assumed.

Same spine as the programme deck, with three changes: slide 2 covers date, length, audience, format, capacity and price; the "inside it" slide becomes the agenda, where every line is a takeaway rather than a topic; and pricing drops out entirely if the workshop is free, replaced by logistics (what attendees need, how to book).

## 3. Partnership deck (8 to 10 slides)

Showing a prospective partner what we have built and proposing a specific trade. The audience is a decision maker who scans: no hype, and every number dated.

Build it from the `partnership-outreach` dossier for that company. The dossier is the only source for anything about their side, and the ask on the trade slide must match the ask in the outreach email, so the deck and the email never disagree.

**Collect first:** our own audience and channel numbers with their as-of dates (`content/business-audit/`, the HQ dashboard extracts), the partner dossier, and the designed trade.

**Slide jobs:**

1. "A-Level Accelerators x [Partner]", and the premise in one line.
2. Who we are in six facts: founder, method, programmes, audience assets, list size with its date, channels.
3. What we have built: the product suite and the free-tool funnel.
4. Who we reach: audience numbers, each carrying its date and source.
5. Why them: what we genuinely rate about their work, and why the audiences fit.
6. The trade: what we bring, what we ask, why it is fair.
7. What the first step looks like: small, concrete, reversible.
8. The decision: a call or a pilot, and Waleed's contact.

Never put revenue projections in a partnership deck, and never a claim about the partner that is not in the sourced dossier.

## Copy voice on slides

Slides carry fragments and statements, not paragraphs. Where a full sentence appears, it is Waleed's spoken voice: direct address, short emphatic sentences, contractions, medical framing where it lands naturally. The founder slide is condensed from his real story on the homepage; never write him a new biography. Anything the deck cannot prove stays off the deck and goes in his mouth instead.
