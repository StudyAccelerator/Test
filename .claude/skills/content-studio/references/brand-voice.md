# Brand voice | Dr Waleed Ahmad / A-Level Accelerators

Read this before writing anything. The goal is that a stranger who has watched Waleed's videos would read the post and think "yes, that's him."

## Who Waleed is (use this, don't fabricate around it)

- UK **foundation-year doctor (MBBS)**. Newly qualified, so he's close in age to his students ("I was a Year 13 student 6 years ago").
- Former **top-performing A-level student** who got into **medicine**. Openly admits his own revision method was bad at first and he succeeded on brute-force hours before learning to work smart. This honesty is central to the brand.
- Has worked with **1,000+ students**. Founder of A-Level Accelerators.
- Teaching philosophy: schools teach you *what* to learn, never *how to perform in an exam*. Grades come from four tiers (knowledge, recall, application, exam mastery) and most students are stuck long before the top.

## How he actually talks (patterns pulled from his workshops, calls and posts)

- **Direct address.** Talks to one student: "Be honest with yourself." "Have a think." "Are you someone who..."
- **Short, punchy sentences for emphasis.** "Not really." "That discomfort is where the marks are made." "Probably not."
- **Confessional and humble.** Admits his own mistakes: "I spent hours making the perfect timetable. Such a waste of time." "My method definitely wasn't good."
- **Contractions everywhere.** "it's", "you're", "didn't", "can't", "I'm". Never "it is not", "do not".
- **Medical framing, lightly.** "I'm a doctor. I can't treat a patient until I know what's wrong. Same with revision." "high-yield vs low-yield." "That's a retrieval failure, not a knowledge failure."
- **Core vocabulary:** high-yield / low-yield, active recall, retrieval, "move the needle", quality over quantity, the four tiers, "exams test recall, not recognition", "knowing something vs performing with it".
- **British English:** whilst, revising, maths, Year 12/13, sat the exam, marks, mark scheme, predicted grades, UCAS.
- **Warm, encouraging close.** "Keep going!" "You're in the right place." "Follow along. I share everything I wish someone had told me at 17."

## Hard rules | anti-AI-tells (these make or break authenticity)

The user has repeatedly and firmly required these. Breaking them fails the task.

1. **No em dashes or en dashes. Ever.** Not the long dash, not the medium one. Use a full stop, a comma, or a colon. Write ranges as "3 to 5", never with a dash.
2. **No curly/smart quotes in anything that will be pasted into code.** Straight quotes only.
3. **Banned AI vocabulary:** delve, tapestry, pivotal, crucial, robust, landscape, testament, underscore, leverage, seamless, comprehensive, foster, garner, vibrant, meticulous, "in today's fast-paced world", "when it comes to", "the world of", unlock, elevate, game-changing, "dive in", navigate (metaphorical), "it's not just X, it's Y", "look no further".
4. **No rule-of-three padding** ("clear, concise, and compelling"). No bolded-label list items as the whole post.
5. **Contractions on. Formal phrasing off.**
6. **Don't manufacture statistics or results.** Approved proof only (see audience-and-offers.md).

## Voice by audience

**To students** (TikTok, Reels, Shorts, some LinkedIn): peer-to-peer, faster, funnier, a little self-deprecating. He's the older brother who became a doctor and will tell you the truth. Can open with a callout: "You're revising wrong and I can prove it in 30 seconds."

**To parents** (Facebook, parent-facing LinkedIn): calm, credible, reassuring. Lead with the doctor credibility and the reassurance that effort isn't the problem. Never talk down. The subtext is always: your hard-working child isn't failing, they were never taught the system, and that's fixable.

## Signature opening moves (rotate, don't repeat)

- A confession: "I got into medicine with a revision method that was genuinely terrible."
- A myth to kill: "The most dangerous revision advice every exam season is 'just do more past papers'."
- A felt moment: "That feeling in the exam hall. 'I knew this yesterday. Where's it gone?'"
- A parent reframe: "If your child is working hard and still stuck at a B, effort isn't the problem."
- A specific number or scene: "Six years ago I sat where your child is sitting."
- A direct question (sparingly, it's been overused): "Be honest. Which one is you?"

## Sign-offs

Students: "Follow for the stuff school never taught you." / "Save this for your next session."
Parents: "Any questions, just message me. I'm always happy to talk it through."
